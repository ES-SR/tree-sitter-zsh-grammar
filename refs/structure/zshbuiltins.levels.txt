1 0 SHELL BUILTIN COMMANDS
2 5 Some shell builtin commands take options as described in individual
2 5 entries; these are often referred to in the list below as ‘flags' to avoid
2 5 confusion with shell options, which may also have an effect on the
2 5 behaviour of builtin commands.  In this introductory section, ‘option'
2 5 always has the meaning of an option to a command that should be familiar
2 5 to most command line users.
2 5 Typically, options are single letters preceded by a hyphen (-).  Options
2 5 that take an argument accept it either immediately following the option
2 5 letter or after white space, for example ‘print -C3 {1..9}' or ‘print -C 3
2 5 {1..9}' are equivalent.  Arguments to options are not the same as
2 5 arguments to the command; the documentation indicates which is which.
2 5 Options that do not take an argument may be combined in a single word, for
2 5 example ‘print -rca -- *' and ‘print -r -c -a -- *' are equivalent.
2 5 Some shell builtin commands also take options that begin with ‘+' instead
2 5 of ‘-'.  The list below makes clear which commands these are.
2 5 Options (together with their individual arguments, if any) must appear in
2 5 a group before any non-option arguments; once the first non-option
2 5 argument has been found, option processing is terminated.
2 5 All builtin commands other than ‘echo' and precommand modifiers, even
2 5 those that have no options, can be given the argument ‘--' to terminate
2 5 option processing.  This indicates that the following words are non-option
2 5 arguments, but is otherwise ignored.  This is useful in cases where
2 5 arguments to the command may begin with ‘-'.  For historical reasons, most
2 5 builtin commands (including ‘echo') also recognize a single ‘-' in a
2 5 separate word for this purpose; note that this is less standard and use of
2 5 ‘--' is recommended.

2 5 . file [ arg ... ]
6 12 Read commands from file and execute them in the current shell
6 12 environment.
6 12 If file does not contain a slash, or if PATH_DIRS is set, the shell
6 12 looks in the components of $path to find the directory containing
6 12 file.  Files in the current directory are not read unless ‘.'
6 12 appears somewhere in $path.  If a file named ‘file.zwc' is found,
6 12 is newer than file, and is the compiled form (created with the
6 12 zcompile builtin) of file, then commands are read from that file
6 12 instead of file.
6 12 If any arguments arg are given, they become the positional
6 12 parameters; the old positional parameters are restored when the
6 12 file is done executing.  However, if no arguments are given, the
6 12 positional parameters remain those of the calling context, and no
6 12 restoring is done.
6 12 If file was not found the return status is 127; if file was found
6 12 but contained a syntax error the return status is 126; else the
6 12 return status is the exit status of the last command executed.
2 5 : [ arg ... ]
6 12 This command does nothing, although normal argument expansions is
6 12 performed which may have effects on shell parameters.  A zero exit
6 12 status is returned.
2 5 alias [ {+|-}gmrsL ] [ name[=value] ... ]
6 12 For each name with a corresponding value, define an alias with that
6 12 value.  A trailing space in value causes the next word to be
6 12 checked for alias expansion.  If the -g flag is present, define a
6 12 global alias; global aliases are expanded even if they do not occur
6 12 in command position:
8 19 % perldoc --help 2>&1 | grep 'built-in functions'
10 23 -f   Search Perl built-in functions
8 19 % alias -g HG='--help 2>&1 | grep'
8 19 % perldoc HG 'built-in functions'
10 23 -f   Search Perl built-in functions
6 12 If the -s flag is present, define a suffix alias: if the command
6 12 word on a command line is in the form ‘text.name', where text is
6 12 any non-empty string, it is replaced by the text ‘value text.name'.
6 12 Note that name is treated as a literal string, not a pattern.  A
6 12 trailing space in value is not special in this case.  For example,
8 19 alias -s ps='gv --'
6 12 will cause the command ‘*.ps' to be expanded to ‘gv -- *.ps'.  As
6 12 alias expansion is carried out earlier than globbing, the ‘*.ps'
6 12 will then be expanded.  Suffix aliases constitute a different name
6 12 space from other aliases (so in the above example it is still
6 12 possible to create an alias for the command ps) and the two sets
6 12 are never listed together.
6 12 For each name with no value, print the value of name, if any.  With
6 12 no arguments, print all currently defined aliases other than suffix
6 12 aliases.  If the -m flag is given the arguments are taken as
6 12 patterns (they should be quoted to preserve them from being
6 12 interpreted as glob patterns), and the aliases matching these
6 12 patterns are printed.  When printing aliases and one of the -g, -r
6 12 or -s flags is present, restrict the printing to global, regular or
6 12 suffix aliases, respectively; a regular alias is one which is
6 12 neither a global nor a suffix alias.   Using ‘+' instead of ‘-', or
6 12 ending the option list with a single ‘+', prevents the values of
6 12 the aliases from being printed.
6 12 If the -L flag is present, then print each alias in a manner
6 12 suitable for putting in a startup script.  The exit status is
6 12 nonzero if a name (with no value) is given for which no alias has
6 12 been defined.
6 12 For more on aliases, include common problems, see the section
6 12 ALIASING in zshmisc(1).
2 5 autoload [ {+|-}RTUXdkmrtWz ] [ -w ] [ name ... ]
6 12 See the section ‘Autoloading Functions' in zshmisc(1) for full
6 12 details.  The fpath parameter will be searched to find the function
6 12 definition when the function is first referenced.
6 12 If name consists of an absolute path, the function is defined to
6 12 load from the file given (searching as usual for dump files in the
6 12 given location).  The name of the function is the basename
6 12 (non-directory part) of the file.  It is normally an error if the
6 12 function is not found in the given location; however, if the option
6 12 -d is given, searching for the function defaults to $fpath.  If a
6 12 function is loaded by absolute path, any functions loaded from it
6 12 that are marked for autoload without an absolute path have the load
6 12 path of the parent function temporarily prepended to $fpath.
6 12 If the option -r or -R is given, the function is searched for
6 12 immediately and the location is recorded internally for use when
6 12 the function is executed; a relative path is expanded using the
6 12 value of $PWD.  This protects against a change to $fpath after the
6 12 call to autoload.  With -r, if the function is not found, it is
6 12 silently left unresolved until execution; with -R, an error message
6 12 is printed and command processing aborted immediately the search
6 12 fails, i.e. at the autoload command rather than at function
6 12 execution..
6 12 The flag -X may be used only inside a shell function.  It causes
6 12 the calling function to be marked for autoloading and then
6 12 immediately loaded and executed, with the current array of
6 12 positional parameters as arguments.  This replaces the previous
6 12 definition of the function.  If no function definition is found, an
6 12 error is printed and the function remains undefined and marked for
6 12 autoloading.  If an argument is given, it is used as a directory
6 12 (i.e. it does not include the name of the function) in which the
6 12 function is to be found; this may be combined with the -d option to
6 12 allow the function search to default to $fpath if it is not in the
6 12 given location.
6 12 The flag +X attempts to load each name as an autoloaded function,
6 12 but does not execute it.  The exit status is zero (success) if the
6 12 function was not previously defined and a definition for it was
6 12 found.  This does not replace any existing definition of the
6 12 function.  The exit status is nonzero (failure) if the function was
6 12 already defined or when no definition was found.  In the latter
6 12 case the function remains undefined and marked for autoloading.  If
6 12 ksh-style autoloading is enabled, the function created will contain
6 12 the contents of the file plus a call to the function itself
6 12 appended to it, thus giving normal ksh autoloading behaviour on the
6 12 first call to the function.  If the -m flag is also given each name
6 12 is treated as a pattern and all functions already marked for
6 12 autoload that match the pattern are loaded.
6 12 With the -t flag, turn on execution tracing; with -T, turn on
6 12 execution tracing only for the current function, turning it off on
6 12 entry to any called functions that do not also have tracing
6 12 enabled.
6 12 With the -U flag, alias expansion is suppressed when the function
6 12 is loaded.
6 12 With the -w flag, the names are taken as names of files compiled
6 12 with the zcompile builtin, and all functions defined in them are
6 12 marked for autoloading.
6 12 The flags -z and -k mark the function to be autoloaded using the
6 12 zsh or ksh style, as if the option KSH_AUTOLOAD were unset or were
6 12 set, respectively.  The flags override the setting of the option at
6 12 the time the function is loaded.
6 12 Note that the autoload command makes no attempt to ensure the shell
6 12 options set during the loading or execution of the file have any
6 12 particular value.  For this, the emulate command can be used:
8 19 emulate zsh -c 'autoload -Uz func'
6 12 arranges that when func is loaded the shell is in native zsh
6 12 emulation, and this emulation is also applied when func is run.
6 12 Some of the functions of autoload are also provided by functions -u
6 12 or functions -U, but autoload is a more comprehensive interface.

2 5 cd [ -qsLP ] [ arg ]
2 5 cd [ -qsLP ] old new
2 5 cd [ -qsLP ] {+|-}n
6 12 Change the current directory.  In the first form, change the
6 12 current directory to arg, or to the value of $HOME if arg is not
6 12 specified.  If arg is ‘-', change to the previous directory.
6 12 Otherwise, if arg begins with a slash, attempt to change to the
6 12 directory given by arg.
6 12 If arg does not begin with a slash, the behaviour depends on
6 12 whether the current directory ‘.' occurs in the list of directories
6 12 contained in the shell parameter cdpath.  If it does not, first
6 12 attempt to change to the directory arg under the current directory,
6 12 and if that fails but cdpath is set and contains at least one
6 12 element attempt to change to the directory arg under each component
6 12 of cdpath in turn until successful.  If ‘.' occurs in cdpath, then
6 12 cdpath is searched strictly in order so that ‘.' is only tried at
6 12 the appropriate point.
6 12 The order of testing cdpath is modified if the option POSIX_CD is
6 12 set, as described in the documentation for the option.
6 12 If no directory is found, the option CDABLE_VARS is set, and a
6 12 parameter named arg exists whose value begins with a slash, treat
6 12 its value as the directory.  In that case, the parameter is added
6 12 to the named directory hash table.
6 12 The second form of cd substitutes the string new for the string old
6 12 in the name of the current directory, and tries to change to this
6 12 new directory.
6 12 The third form of cd extracts an entry from the directory stack,
6 12 and changes to that directory.  An argument of the form ‘+n'
6 12 identifies a stack entry by counting from the left of the list
6 12 shown by the dirs command, starting with zero.  An argument of the
6 12 form ‘-n' counts from the right.  If the PUSHD_MINUS option is set,
6 12 the meanings of ‘+' and ‘-' in this context are swapped.  If the
6 12 POSIX_CD option is set, this form of cd is not recognised and will
6 12 be interpreted as the first form.
6 12 If the -q (quiet) option is specified, the hook function chpwd and
6 12 the functions in the array chpwd_functions are not called.  This is
6 12 useful for calls to cd that do not change the environment seen by
6 12 an interactive user.
6 12 If the -s option is specified, cd refuses to change the current
6 12 directory if the given pathname contains symlinks.  If the -P
6 12 option is given or the CHASE_LINKS option is set, symbolic links
6 12 are resolved to their true values.  If the -L option is given
6 12 symbolic links are retained in the directory (and not resolved)
6 12 regardless of the state of the CHASE_LINKS option.

2 5 disable [ -afmprs ] name ...
6 12 Temporarily disable the named hash table elements or patterns.  The
6 12 default is to disable builtin commands.  This allows you to use an
6 12 external command with the same name as a builtin command.  The -a
6 12 option causes disable to act on regular or global aliases.  The -s
6 12 option causes disable to act on suffix aliases.  The -f option
6 12 causes disable to act on shell functions.  The -r options causes
6 12 disable to act on reserved words.  Without arguments all disabled
6 12 hash table elements from the corresponding hash table are printed.
6 12 With the -m flag the arguments are taken as patterns (which should
6 12 be quoted to prevent them from undergoing filename expansion), and
6 12 all hash table elements from the corresponding hash table matching
6 12 these patterns are disabled.  Disabled objects can be enabled with
6 12 the enable command.
6 12 With the option -p, name ... refer to elements of the shell's
6 12 pattern syntax as described in the section ‘Filename Generation'.
6 12 Certain elements can be disabled separately, as given below.
6 12 Note that patterns not allowed by the current settings for the
6 12 options EXTENDED_GLOB, KSH_GLOB and SH_GLOB are never enabled,
6 12 regardless of the setting here.  For example, if EXTENDED_GLOB is
6 12 not active, the pattern ^ is ineffective even if ‘disable -p "^"'
6 12 has not been issued.  The list below indicates any option settings
6 12 that restrict the use of the pattern.  It should be noted that
6 12 setting SH_GLOB has a wider effect than merely disabling patterns
6 12 as certain expressions, in particular those involving parentheses,
6 12 are parsed differently.
6 12 The following patterns may be disabled; all the strings need
6 12 quoting on the command line to prevent them from being interpreted
6 12 immediately as patterns and the patterns are shown below in single
6 12 quotes as a reminder.
6 12 '?'    The pattern character ? wherever it occurs, including when
8 19 preceding a parenthesis with KSH_GLOB.
6 12 '*'    The pattern character * wherever it occurs, including
8 19 recursive globbing and when preceding a parenthesis with
8 19 KSH_GLOB.
6 12 '['    Character classes.
6 12 '<' (NO_SH_GLOB)
8 19 Numeric ranges.
6 12 '|' (NO_SH_GLOB)
8 19 Alternation in grouped patterns, case statements, or
8 19 KSH_GLOB parenthesised expressions.
6 12 '(' (NO_SH_GLOB)
8 19 Grouping using single parentheses.  Disabling this does not
8 19 disable the use of parentheses for KSH_GLOB where they are
8 19 introduced by a special character, nor for glob qualifiers
8 19 (use ‘setopt NO_BARE_GLOB_QUAL' to disable glob qualifiers
8 19 that use parentheses only).
6 12 '~' (EXTENDED_GLOB)
8 19 Exclusion in the form A~B.
6 12 '^' (EXTENDED_GLOB)
8 19 Exclusion in the form A^B.
6 12 '#' (EXTENDED_GLOB)
8 19 The pattern character # wherever it occurs, both for
8 19 repetition of a previous pattern and for indicating globbing
8 19 flags.
6 12 '?(' (KSH_GLOB)
8 19 The grouping form ?(...).  Note this is also disabled if '?'
8 19 is disabled.
6 12 '*(' (KSH_GLOB)
8 19 The grouping form *(...).  Note this is also disabled if '*'
8 19 is disabled.
6 12 '+(' (KSH_GLOB)
8 19 The grouping form +(...).
6 12 '!(' (KSH_GLOB)
8 19 The grouping form !(...).
6 12 '@(' (KSH_GLOB)
8 19 The grouping form @(...).

2 5 echo [ -neE ] [ arg ... ]
6 12 Write each arg on the standard output, with a space separating each
6 12 one.  If the -n flag is not present, print a newline at the end.
6 12 echo recognizes the following escape sequences:
6 12 \a     bell character
6 12 \b     backspace
6 12 \c     suppress subsequent characters and final newline
6 12 \e     escape
6 12 \f     form feed
6 12 \n     linefeed (newline)
6 12 \r     carriage return
6 12 \t     horizontal tab
6 12 \v     vertical tab
6 12 \\     backslash
6 12 \0NNN  character code in octal
6 12 \xNN   character code in hexadecimal
6 12 \uNNNN
8 19 unicode character code in hexadecimal
6 12 \UNNNNNNNN
8 19 unicode character code in hexadecimal
6 12 The -E flag, or the BSD_ECHO option, can be used to disable these
6 12 escape sequences.  In the latter case, -e flag can be used to
6 12 enable them.
6 12 Note that for standards compliance a double dash does not terminate
6 12 option processing; instead, it is printed directly.  However, a
6 12 single dash does terminate option processing, so the first dash,
6 12 possibly following options, is not printed, but everything
6 12 following it is printed as an argument.  The single dash behaviour
6 12 is different from other shells.  For a more portable way of
6 12 printing text, see printf, and for a more controllable way of
6 12 printing text within zsh, see print.

2 5 emulate [ -lLR ] [ {zsh|sh|ksh|csh} [ flags ... ] ]
6 12 Without any argument print current emulation mode.
6 12 With single argument set up zsh options to emulate the specified
6 12 shell as much as possible.  csh will never be fully emulated.  If
6 12 the argument is not one of the shells listed above, zsh will be
6 12 used as a default; more precisely, the tests performed on the
6 12 argument are the same as those used to determine the emulation at
6 12 startup based on the shell name, see the section COMPATIBILITY in
6 12 zsh(1) .  In addition to setting shell options, the command also
6 12 restores the pristine state of pattern enables, as if all patterns
6 12 had been enabled using enable -p.
6 12 If the emulate command occurs inside a function that has been
6 12 marked for execution tracing with functions -t then the xtrace
6 12 option will be turned on regardless of emulation mode or other
6 12 options.  Note that code executed inside the function by the .,
6 12 source, or eval commands is not considered to be running directly
6 12 from the function, hence does not provoke this behaviour.
6 12 If the -R switch is given, all settable options are reset to their
6 12 default value corresponding to the specified emulation mode, except
6 12 for certain options describing the interactive environment;
6 12 otherwise, only those options likely to cause portability problems
6 12 in scripts and functions are altered.  If the -L switch is given,
6 12 the options LOCAL_OPTIONS, LOCAL_PATTERNS and LOCAL_TRAPS will be
6 12 set as well, causing the effects of the emulate command and any
6 12 setopt, disable -p or enable -p, and trap commands to be local to
6 12 the immediately surrounding shell function, if any; normally these
6 12 options are turned off in all emulation modes except ksh. The -L
6 12 switch is mutually exclusive with the use of -c in flags.
6 12 If there is a single argument and the -l switch is given, the
6 12 options that would be set or unset (the latter indicated with the
6 12 prefix ‘no') are listed.  -l can be combined with -L or -R and the
6 12 list will be modified in the appropriate way.  Note the list does
6 12 not depend on the current setting of options, i.e. it includes all
6 12 options that may in principle change, not just those that would
6 12 actually change.
6 12 The flags may be any of the invocation-time flags described in the
6 12 section INVOCATION in zsh(1), except that ‘-o EMACS' and ‘-o VI'
6 12 may not be used.  Flags such as ‘+r'/‘+o RESTRICTED' may be
6 12 prohibited in some circumstances.
6 12 If -c arg appears in flags, arg is evaluated while the requested
6 12 emulation is temporarily in effect.  In this case the emulation
6 12 mode and all options are restored to their previous values before
6 12 emulate returns.  The -R switch may precede the name of the shell
6 12 to emulate; note this has a meaning distinct from including -R in
6 12 flags.
6 12 Use of -c enables ‘sticky' emulation mode for functions defined
6 12 within the evaluated expression:  the emulation mode is associated
6 12 thereafter with the function so that whenever the function is
6 12 executed the emulation (respecting the -R switch, if present) and
6 12 all options are set (and pattern disables cleared) before entry to
6 12 the function, and the state is restored after exit.  If the
6 12 function is called when the sticky emulation is already in effect,
6 12 either within an ‘emulate shell -c' expression or within another
6 12 function with the same sticky emulation, entry and exit from the
6 12 function do not cause options to be altered (except due to standard
6 12 processing such as the LOCAL_OPTIONS option).  This also applies to
6 12 functions marked for autoload within the sticky emulation; the
6 12 appropriate set of options will be applied at the point the
6 12 function is loaded as well as when it is run.
6 12 For example:
8 19 emulate sh -c 'fni() { setopt cshnullglob; }
8 19 fno() { fni; }'
8 19 fno
6 12 The two functions fni and fno are defined with sticky sh emulation.
6 12 fno is then executed, causing options associated with emulations to
6 12 be set to their values in sh.  fno then calls fni; because fni is
6 12 also marked for sticky sh emulation, no option changes take place
6 12 on entry to or exit from it.  Hence the option cshnullglob, turned
6 12 off by sh emulation, will be turned on within fni and remain on
6 12 return to fno.  On exit from fno, the emulation mode and all
6 12 options will be restored to the state they were in before entry to
6 12 the temporary emulation.
6 12 The documentation above is typically sufficient for the intended
6 12 purpose of executing code designed for other shells in a suitable
6 12 environment.  More detailed rules follow.
6 12 1.     The sticky emulation environment provided by ‘emulate shell
8 19 -c' is identical to that provided by entry to a function
8 19 marked for sticky emulation as a consequence of being
8 19 defined in such an environment.  Hence, for example, the
8 19 sticky emulation is inherited by subfunctions defined within
8 19 functions with sticky emulation.
6 12 2.     No change of options takes place on entry to or exit from
8 19 functions that are not marked for sticky emulation, other
8 19 than those that would normally take place, even if those
8 19 functions are called within sticky emulation.
6 12 3.     No special handling is provided for functions marked for
8 19 autoload nor for functions present in wordcode created by
8 19 the zcompile command.
6 12 4.     The presence or absence of the -R switch to emulate
8 19 corresponds to different sticky emulation modes, so for
8 19 example ‘emulate sh -c', ‘emulate -R sh -c' and ‘emulate csh
8 19 -c' are treated as three distinct sticky emulations.
6 12 5.     Difference in shell options supplied in addition to the
8 19 basic emulation also mean the sticky emulations are
8 19 different, so for example ‘emulate zsh -c' and ‘emulate zsh
8 19 -o cbases -c' are treated as distinct sticky emulations.
2 5 enable [ -afmprs ] name ...
6 12 Enable the named hash table elements, presumably disabled earlier
6 12 with disable.  The default is to enable builtin commands.  The -a
6 12 option causes enable to act on regular or global aliases.  The -s
6 12 option causes enable to act on suffix aliases.  The -f option
6 12 causes enable to act on shell functions.  The -r option causes
6 12 enable to act on reserved words.  Without arguments all enabled
6 12 hash table elements from the corresponding hash table are printed.
6 12 With the -m flag the arguments are taken as patterns (should be
6 12 quoted) and all hash table elements from the corresponding hash
6 12 table matching these patterns are enabled.  Enabled objects can be
6 12 disabled with the disable builtin command.
6 12 enable -p reenables patterns disabled with disable -p.  Note that
6 12 it does not override globbing options; for example, ‘enable -p "~"'
6 12 does not cause the pattern character ~ to be active unless the
6 12 EXTENDED_GLOB option is also set.  To enable all possible patterns
6 12 (so that they may be individually disabled with disable -p), use
6 12 ‘setopt EXTENDED_GLOB KSH_GLOB NO_SH_GLOB'.

6 12 hash can be used to directly modify the contents of the command

6 12 functions and sourcing of scripts by avoiding parsing of the text

6 12 how autoloaded functions are searched.  The extension .zwc stands
6 12 for ‘zsh word code'.

6 12 compiled into the output file given as the first argument.  If file
6 12 does not end in .zwc, this extension is automatically appended.
6 12 Files containing multiple compiled functions are called ‘digest'
6 12 files, and are intended to be used as elements of the FPATH/fpath
6 12 special array.

6 12 Performs operations relating to zsh's loadable modules.  Loading of
6 12 modules while the shell is running (‘dynamical loading') is not
6 12 available on all operating systems, or on all installations on a
6 12 particular operating system, although the zmodload command itself
6 12 is always available and can be used to manipulate modules built
6 12 into versions of the shell executable without dynamical loading.
6 12 Without arguments the names of all currently loaded binary modules
6 12 are printed.  The -L option causes this list to be in the form of a
6 12 series of zmodload commands.  Forms with arguments are:
6 12 zmodload [ -is ] name ...
6 12 zmodload -u [ -i ] name ...
8 19 In the simplest case, zmodload loads a binary module.  The
8 19 module must be in a file with a name consisting of the
8 19 specified name followed by a standard suffix, usually ‘.so'
8 19 (‘.sl' on HPUX).  If the module to be loaded is already
8 19 loaded the duplicate module is ignored.  If zmodload detects
8 19 an inconsistency, such as an invalid module name or circular
8 19 dependency list, the current code block is aborted.  If it
8 19 is available, the module is loaded if necessary, while if it
8 19 is not available, non-zero status is silently returned.  The
8 19 option -i is accepted for compatibility but has no effect.
8 19 The named module is searched for in the same way a command
8 19 is, using $module_path instead of $path.  However, the path
8 19 search is performed even when the module name contains a
8 19 ‘/', which it usually does.  There is no way to prevent the
8 19 path search.
8 19 If the module supports features (see below), zmodload tries
8 19 to enable all features when loading a module.  If the module
8 19 was successfully loaded but not all features could be
8 19 enabled, zmodload returns status 2.
8 19 If the option -s is given, no error is printed if the module
8 19 was not available (though other errors indicating a problem
8 19 with the module are printed).  The return status indicates
8 19 if the module was loaded.  This is appropriate if the caller
8 19 considers the module optional.
8 19 With -u, zmodload unloads modules.  The same name must be
8 19 given that was given when the module was loaded, but it is
8 19 not necessary for the module to exist in the file system.
8 19 The -i option suppresses the error if the module is already
8 19 unloaded (or was never loaded).
8 19 Each module has a boot and a cleanup function.  The module
8 19 will not be loaded if its boot function fails.  Similarly a
8 19 module can only be unloaded if its cleanup function runs
8 19 successfully.
6 12 zmodload -F [ -almLe -P param ] module [ [+-]feature ... ]
8 19 zmodload -F allows more selective control over the features
8 19 provided by modules.  With no options apart from -F, the
8 19 module named module is loaded, if it was not already loaded,
8 19 and the list of features is set to the required state.  If
8 19 no features are specified, the module is loaded, if it was
8 19 not already loaded, but the state of features is unchanged.
8 19 Each feature may be preceded by a + to turn the feature on,
8 19 or - to turn it off; the + is assumed if neither character
8 19 is present.  Any feature not explicitly mentioned is left in
8 19 its current state; if the module was not previously loaded
8 19 this means any such features will remain disabled.  The
8 19 return status is zero if all features were set, 1 if the
8 19 module failed to load, and 2 if some features could not be
8 19 set (for example, a parameter couldn't be added because
8 19 there was a different parameter of the same name) but the
8 19 module was loaded.
8 19 The standard features are builtins, conditions, parameters
8 19 and math functions; these are indicated by the prefix ‘b:',
8 19 ‘c:' (‘C:' for an infix condition), ‘p:' and ‘f:',
8 19 respectively, followed by the name that the corresponding
8 19 feature would have in the shell.  For example, ‘b:strftime'
8 19 indicates a builtin named strftime and p:EPOCHSECONDS
8 19 indicates a parameter named EPOCHSECONDS.  The module may
8 19 provide other (‘abstract') features of its own as indicated
8 19 by its documentation; these have no prefix.
8 19 With -l or -L, features provided by the module are listed.
8 19 With -l alone, a list of features together with their states
8 19 is shown, one feature per line.  With -L alone, a zmodload
8 19 -F command that would cause enabled features of the module
8 19 to be turned on is shown.  With -lL, a zmodload -F command
8 19 that would cause all the features to be set to their current
8 19 state is shown.  If one of these combinations is given with
8 19 the option -P param then the parameter param is set to an
8 19 array of features, either features together with their state
8 19 or (if -L alone is given) enabled features.
8 19 With the option -L the module name may be omitted; then a
8 19 list of all enabled features for all modules providing
8 19 features is printed in the form of zmodload -F commands.  If
8 19 -l is also given, the state of both enabled and disabled
8 19 features is output in that form.
8 19 A set of features may be provided together with -l or -L and
8 19 a module name; in that case only the state of those features
8 19 is considered.  Each feature may be preceded by + or - but
8 19 the character has no effect.  If no set of features is
8 19 provided, all features are considered.
8 19 With -e, the command first tests that the module is loaded;
8 19 if it is not, status 1 is returned.  If the module is
8 19 loaded, the list of features given as an argument is
8 19 examined.  Any feature given with no prefix is simply tested
8 19 to see if the module provides it; any feature given with a
8 19 prefix + or - is tested to see if is provided and in the
8 19 given state.  If the tests on all features in the list
8 19 succeed, status 0 is returned, else status 1.
8 19 With -m, each entry in the given list of features is taken
8 19 as a pattern to be matched against the list of features
8 19 provided by the module.  An initial + or - must be given
8 19 explicitly.  This may not be combined with the -a option as
8 19 autoloads must be specified explicitly.
8 19 With -a, the given list of features is marked for autoload
8 19 from the specified module, which may not yet be loaded.  An
8 19 optional + may appear before the feature name.  If the
8 19 feature is prefixed with -, any existing autoload is
8 19 removed.  The options -l and -L may be used to list
8 19 autoloads.  Autoloading is specific to individual features;
8 19 when the module is loaded only the requested feature is
8 19 enabled.  Autoload requests are preserved if the module is
8 19 subsequently unloaded until an explicit ‘zmodload -Fa module
8 19 -feature' is issued.  It is not an error to request an
8 19 autoload for a feature of a module that is already loaded.
8 19 When the module is loaded each autoload is checked against
8 19 the features actually provided by the module; if the feature
8 19 is not provided the autoload request is deleted.  A warning
8 19 message is output; if the module is being loaded to provide
8 19 a different feature, and that autoload is successful, there
8 19 is no effect on the status of the current command.  If the
8 19 module is already loaded at the time when zmodload -Fa is
8 19 run, an error message is printed and status 1 returned.
8 19 zmodload -Fa can be used with the -l, -L, -e and -P options
8 19 for listing and testing the existence of autoloadable
8 19 features.  In this case -l is ignored if -L is specified.
8 19 zmodload -FaL with no module name lists autoloads for all
8 19 modules.
8 19 Note that only standard features as described above can be
8 19 autoloaded; other features require the module to be loaded
8 19 before enabling.
6 12 zmodload -d [ -L ] [ name ]
6 12 zmodload -d name dep ...
6 12 zmodload -ud name [ dep ... ]
8 19 The -d option can be used to specify module dependencies.
8 19 The modules named in the second and subsequent arguments
8 19 will be loaded before the module named in the first
8 19 argument.
8 19 With -d and one argument, all dependencies for that module
8 19 are listed.  With -d and no arguments, all module
8 19 dependencies are listed.  This listing is by default in a
8 19 Makefile-like format.  The -L option changes this format to
8 19 a list of zmodload -d commands.
8 19 If -d and -u are both used, dependencies are removed.  If
8 19 only one argument is given, all dependencies for that module

6 12 zmodload -ab [ -L ]
6 12 zmodload -ab [ -i ] name [ builtin ... ]
6 12 zmodload -ub [ -i ] builtin ...
8 19 The -ab option defines autoloaded builtins.  It defines the
8 19 specified builtins.  When any of those builtins is called,
8 19 the module specified in the first argument is loaded and all
8 19 its features are enabled (for selective control of features
8 19 use ‘zmodload -F -a' as described above).  If only the name
8 19 is given, one builtin is defined, with the same name as the
8 19 module.  -i suppresses the error if the builtin is already
8 19 defined or autoloaded, but not if another builtin of the
8 19 same name is already defined.
8 19 With -ab and no arguments, all autoloaded builtins are
8 19 listed, with the module name (if different) shown in
8 19 parentheses after the builtin name.  The -L option changes
8 19 this format to a list of zmodload -a commands.
8 19 If -b is used together with the -u option, it removes
8 19 builtins previously defined with -ab.  This is only possible
8 19 if the builtin is not yet loaded.  -i suppresses the error
8 19 if the builtin is already removed (or never existed).
8 19 Autoload requests are retained if the module is subsequently
8 19 unloaded until an explicit ‘zmodload -ub builtin' is issued.

6 12 zmodload -e [ -A ] [ string ... ]
8 19 The -e option without arguments lists all loaded modules; if
8 19 the -A option is also given, module aliases corresponding to
8 19 loaded modules are also shown.  If arguments are provided,
8 19 nothing is printed; the return status is set to zero if all
8 19 strings given as arguments are names of loaded modules and
8 19 to one if at least on string is not the name of a loaded
8 19 module.  This can be used to test for the availability of
8 19 things implemented by modules.  In this case, any aliases
8 19 are automatically resolved and the -A flag is not used.
6 12 zmodload -A [ -L ] [ modalias[=module] ... ]
8 19 For each argument, if both modalias and module are given,
8 19 define modalias to be an alias for the module module.  If
8 19 the module modalias is ever subsequently requested, either
8 19 via a call to zmodload or implicitly, the shell will attempt
8 19 to load module instead.  If module is not given, show the
8 19 definition of modalias.  If no arguments are given, list all
8 19 defined module aliases.  When listing, if the -L flag was
8 19 also given, list the definition as a zmodload command to
8 19 recreate the alias.
8 19 The existence of aliases for modules is completely
8 19 independent of whether the name resolved is actually loaded
8 19 as a module: while the alias exists, loading and unloading
8 19 the module under any alias has exactly the same effect as
8 19 using the resolved name, and does not affect the connection
8 19 between the alias and the resolved name which can be removed
8 19 either by zmodload -R or by redefining the alias.  Chains of
8 19 aliases (i.e. where the first resolved name is itself an
8 19 alias) are valid so long as these are not circular.  As the
8 19 aliases take the same format as module names, they may
8 19 include path separators:  in this case, there is no
8 19 requirement for any part of the path named to exist as the
8 19 alias will be resolved first.  For example, ‘any/old/alias'
8 19 is always a valid alias.
8 19 Dependencies added to aliased modules are actually added to
8 19 the resolved module; these remain if the alias is removed.
8 19 It is valid to create an alias whose name is one of the
8 19 standard shell modules and which resolves to a different
8 19 module.  However, if a module has dependencies, it will not
8 19 be possible to use the module name as an alias as the module
8 19 will already be marked as a loadable module in its own
8 19 right.
8 19 Apart from the above, aliases can be used in the zmodload
8 19 command anywhere module names are required.  However,
8 19 aliases will not be shown in lists of loaded modules with a
8 19 bare ‘zmodload'.
6 12 zmodload -R modalias ...
8 19 For each modalias argument that was previously defined as a
8 19 module alias via zmodload -A, delete the alias.  If any was
8 19 not defined, an error is caused and the remainder of the
8 19 line is ignored.
